Bluestem Workingpersons 11 Features

  • Walnut four layer 11” by 2-3/4” by 7/16” rim
  • 3/8” by 3/16” raw brass tone ring
  • 11” high crown Remo Renaissance head
  • 14 notch 1/2” by 1/8” Patina finished raw brass tension band
  • 14 patina finished raw brass shoes attached with stainless steel bolts and flat washers
  • 14 standard 2-3/4” length nickel plated hooks and Vega style open end 5/16” nuts
  • Walnut 3 piece laminated neck with 1/8" cherry center stripe
  • Neck reinforced with 2024-T4 aircraft aluminum bar
  • 24" scale length 12” radius ebony fret board
  • Ebony peg head overlay with simple mother of pearl torch inlay
  • for peg head
  • Five 3/16” mother of pearl fret board position markers
  • 3/8” mother of pearl snowflake inlay at fifth fret
  • Custom 12th fret inlay demonstrates simple custom inlay design and engraving techniques
  • Small inlaid position markers at side of fret board
  • Tunneled fifth string housed in stainless steel micro tubing
  • 17 medium EVO gold frets plus two partial frets across ogee scoop at heel
  • Zero fret with 1/8” bone string spacer interchangeably accommodates steel or synthetic strings
  • Grover Sta-tite gold plated Deluxe individual tuners arranged in 3+2 configuration
  • Dobson-style neck heel with embedded 1/4” by 20 by 1-1/4” long barrel nut for solid attachment to pot
  • Neck attached by 1/4” x 20 threaded rod and brass acorn nut
  • Channeled dowel stick 1” square at heel and tapering to 3/4” square at tail piece allows easy action adjustment
  • Custom fabricated raw brass adjustable tension tailpiece and stainless steel end bracket
  • Custom bridge composed of two layers of quarter sawn violin grade maple over ebony center section

INTRODUCTION

The plan and photos are designed as a tour through the construction process I used for constructing the Bluestem Workingpersons 11 slot head open back banjo. The banjo detailed here is purposefully designed to facilitate easy construction, modification, and action adjustment. This guide is meant to provide a bit of additional information for the sequential photo documentation of the build, but I’ll also use it to offer advice on alternative methods to some of the processes used as well as providing information for modification of the basic design should that be desired.

The plan itself details a slot head open back banjo using an 11” pot, 24” scale length, and 1-3/8" neck width at zero fret(or nut), and tunneled fifth string. The dimensions are based on what my personal preferences are, but the design is easily modified to please those who prefer a different pot size, longer scale or different bridge position on the head.

A WORD ABOUT THE DESIGN

This design is based on my personal preferences in a relatively unadorned and solidly constructed instrument that can easily accommodate changing common features such as string type or switching to a hide head without major modification. The basic platform is also easily adaptable to other preferences such as pot size, neck width at the nut, shorter or longer scale length, or bridge position on the head.

This banjo is loosely based on the Dobson design, but it has been heavily modified from the original design to satisfy my preferences. As an example, this banjo design features a method of neck attachment that does not require a permanently attached dowel stick. This greatly simplifies any heel shaping that is necessary for initial construction and allows easy modification of the neck angle at any time in the future. The neck attachment method also permits string height over the upper frets to be adjusted in less than a minute’s time by loosening the single acorn nut at the tailpiece. A 1-3/8” width neck coupled with a 12” radius fret board and tunneled fifth string and 24” scale length are also pretty unconventional. Once you play one you’ll wonder why, though. The list goes on from there, more on those design points a bit later.

WHY THE PICTORIAL FORMAT?

The photos detail a few of the techniques I’ve developed over many years of building instruments. I wish there would have been something similar when I first started building, but there were few resources available other than a few books on guitar or violin family instrument construction, and the web was still several years in the future. I can only make so many instruments in my lifetime, so this is a way to pass on what I have learned so others can use it to increase the number of great banjos in the world. The guide shows processes that I use, but other methods can be equally effective based on the skill set and tools that are at the disposal of someone else. I use what I have and what I’m comfortable with; review the material presented here and adapt the process to what is best for you.

BEFORE YOU START…

You need to understand that woodworking is a potentially dangerous endeavor!

This seems to be a simple obligatory statement on the surface, but please take a moment to consider its importance. Do not negate the value of working in a safe manner any time you work with either hand or power tools. Secure your work and do not position yourself in a manner that would permit a cutting edge to come in contact with you. If you have any doubt, take the time to change your ergonomics. A moment of negligence can result in personal injury or ruined work, so it is far better to plan out your work methods and use hand and power tools in a way that ensures safety and give you the best chance for producing an instrument that you can display proudly.

My standard safety disclaimer:

I accept NO responsibility for any damage or injury that could occur as a result of following any of the procedures outlined by this guide. Please remember that woodworking is a potentially dangerous endeavor. The machines used to work with wood, metal, and other materials can be hazardous if used in an unsafe manner. Please read all machinery operation manuals and follow the safe working practices outlined within them.

Personal protective gear you must use:

1. Eye protection in the form of safety glasses and face shield where necessary.

2. Hearing protection is a necessity when power equipment is used. This can take the form of ear plugs or over-the-ear headset style noise reduction.

3. Use protection against inhalation of nuisance dusts and particles. Some wood dusts have been shown to be toxic when inhaled. A good quality cartridge filter with a good seal around the nose and mouth should be close at hand in the shop and used whenever cutting or sanding operations are being performed.

4. Appropriate footwear and clothing should also be a shop priority. Shoes should be equipped with non-slip soles and loose fitting clothing that can become entangled in machinery should be avoided.

One Last Preachy Comment on Workshop Tidiness:

Workshops have a way of accumulating clutter, sawdust, and debris. Starting each new phase in any project with a cleanup regimen will go a long way toward increasing your chances for a safe and productive day.

OK, once the shop is clean, you can roll up your sleeves (literally) and get to work…
