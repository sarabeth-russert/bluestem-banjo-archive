The Bluestem Backstep Open Back Banjo



Two banjos that demonstrate the Backstep design are shown above. At the left is a 23-1/2" scale length asymmetric peg head in spalted maple with conventional fifth string tuner location. To the right is a 25-1/2" scale slotted peg head with tunneled fifth string. As part of the design aesthetic, NO ebony, CITES listed woods, or natural shell products were used in the making of these banjos.

The Bluestem Backstep design combines tasteful styling and modern appointments to create banjos that have a distinctive vintage feel while at the same time eliminating the negative aspects of traditional designs. Over years of researching the banjo's origins and earliest commercial designs, I came to the realization that early manufacturers had initially used great designs for neck to rim mounting, but purposefully moved away from practical mechanical designs to please the growing throngs of purchasers who were attracted to the concept of "heeled" instruments, a direct result of the guitar's growing popularity. A close look at the banjo designs that have predominated for the past 150 years reveals just what they had sacrificed in the name of style. Suffice it to say that the "modern" banjo has lived with the results of that choice for a very long time.

I won't elaborate upon the details of the design modifications here, as it's all been posted by me in Banjo Hangout topics if you care to research it further. I've covered all of the details at one time or another in over 9000 posts, some of them VERY lengthy, over a dozen years of being an active Banjo Hangout Forum participant, and re-publishing it all here isn't a practical reality. There's only so much that can be said to convince hobbyist builders that weak and ineffectual heel attachment virtually guarantees that it's going to be VERY difficult to create an instrument that centers the strings over the heel, creates the ideal back angle, and does so in a solid design that doesn't change pitch when pressure is exerted on the neck.

A detailed overview of the Backstep design were presented in the "Bluestem Backstep Open Back Banjo" Banjo Hangout archived discussion.

My current methodology reflects both my desire to eliminate all of the problems associated with the "modern" means of neck attachment and at the same time satisfy my needs as a banjo player. Make no mistake; the "Rudy Rod" attachment method (detailed elsewhere on this website) has HUGE benefits for builders looking for a more conventional banjo design, but my focus here is producing an instrument that fits MY requirements, and I love the results. No banjo design is going to please everyone, so my suggestion is to experiment and find your own path. I did, and I'm very pleased with the results.

ALL of the Backstep series utilizes a through-neck design that automatically centers the strings over the heel and permit quick and easy adjustment of the neck back angle. Changing the back angle of the neck raises or lowers the height of the strings over the upper portion of the neck (action adjustment) or can be used to accommodate a higher or lower bridge height. It isn't even necessary to slack the strings! The "Range Of Motion" demonstration below visually conveys the concept effectively.

Perhaps an even greater benefit to the builder is that there is NO heel profiling necessary. Initial setup is done by sliding the neck into place and locking the back angle with the single acorn nut. As shown in the video, adjustment of the neck angle takes only a moment, with the strings left at full tension!

Bluestem Backstep Open Back Banjo Features:

Highlights of the Backstep banjo design are shown in the numbered list with each point explained in more detail following the list. This gives me a good vehicle to elaborate on any particular feature. I don't use each feature on every banjo, but this serves to list many of my options for any particular build.
  1. 11" diameter (alternate 12") by 1/2" wall by 3" deep rim made from 1/16" layers of walnut, maple, cherry, or oak
  2. Other figured woods (such as spalted maple or quartersawn oak) are occasionally used as outer rim layer
  3. Rim depth includes 1/4" diameter rolled brass tone ring
  4. Peg head overlay in maple, walnut, cherry, or oak, or 1/16" thick Garolite XX opaque black synthetic
  5. Raw brass patina finished 18 notch tension band (20 notches for 12" rim)
  6. Raw brass patina finished shoes with stainless steel mounting screws and washers
  7. Raw Brass patina finished adjustable down force tail piece accommodates loop or ball end steel or tied synthetic strings
  8. Nickel plated round steel J hooks
  9. Nickel plated steel Vega-style open end nuts
  10. Remo Renaissance high crown head
  11. Rounded rear rim cap of 3/16" thick Garolite XX opaque black synthetic
  12. Neck through design allows quick and easy action adjustment, even under string tension!
  13. Fixed neck skew angle geometry (as a result of the neck through design) assures strings will always center over the neck heel
  14. Pau Ferro fret board (non-CITES listed wood) or 3/16" thick Garolite XX opaque black synthetic
  15. Double Ogee neck scoop
  16. Fifteen full and two partial EVO frets
  17. Zero fret and 1/8" synthetic string guide allow steel or synthetic strings to be used interchangeably
  18. "3 + 2" slot head design (or "3+2" asymmetric peg head with vertical string posts and top ferrules)
  19. Grover Sta-tite Deluxe 18:1 cast base slot head tuners
  20. Tunneled fifth string utilizing stainless steel micro-tubing
  21. Stainless steel micro-saddle over fifth fret
  22. Single spike installed at 7th fret
  23. Neck reinforced with 3/8" thick 2024-T4 aircraft aluminum bar, 3/8" tall at first fret tapering to 5/8" tall at heel
  24. Tasteful 1/8" synthetic neck position markers and 1/16" side dots (no natural shell materials used)
  25. Custom laminated bridge, quartersawn maple outer layers over 1/16" thick Pau Ferro or Garolite XX opaque black synthetic core
  26. Comfortable contoured custom arm rest with 3/16" thick contoured Garolite XX opaque black synthetic top

Details of features listed above:

1. The rim is constructed in a method that ensures a stable and solid design without excessive weight. The design ensures full and resonant tone without the excessive edginess often present in thick rim designs. This is accomplished by using solid 1/16" laminations of solid maple, walnut, cherry, or oak.

2. American-grown native species of hardwoods (maple, walnut, cherry, or oak) are used to produce the extra thick rim lamination layers and are selected to match the neck, resulting in an attractive and tastefully coordinated instrument.

3. The rolled brass ring forms a solid connection point between the head and the rim, allowing the head to vibrate freely without excessive energy being lost to the wood rim. The net result is increased sustain with a focused tone. The combination of rolled brass ring and wood rim has been a perennial favorite of players for a long time, and with good reason.

4. Peg head overlays are done in a variety of 3/32" bookmatched figured woods as well as Ebony-like Garolite XX 1/16" opaque black synthetic material.

5. Tension band is rolled from 1/2" tall by 1/8" thick raw brass and joined with an overlapping plate fastened with four #8-32 stainless steel button head screws. Many small shops use 3/16" brass for tension bands but the additional thickness is not necessary and it results in a band that looks "industrial" and not in keeping with the more refined look of a good open back banjo design. Eighteen tension points are plenty, higher numbers aren't necessary and are undesirable in many cases. See my open back design page for further information relating to the hook count numbers game.

6. The raw brass shoes are fabricated and hand finished to provide a superior shoe design. The shoe mounting surface is 3/8" wide and 1" tall to resist heeling into the rim surface as can be seen in shoes with a smaller mounting surface area; solid brass is also much stronger than cast alloy material. The shoes are mounted with #10 by 32 stainless steel screws matched with stainless steel washers.

7. The raw brass tail piece features adjustable down force by means of the two #6 by 32 stainless steel button head screws at the top. The overall height of the tail piece can also be adjusted by the use of the stainless steel #10 by 32 tail piece bracket mounting screw. The Art Deco styled top plate is fanned so the strings progress to the bridge at a relaxed angle. The string mounting holes are designed so loop or ball end steel strings can be used. The design also allows use of synthetic strings retained by a simple multiple wrap of the string end through the top of the string loop.

8. Nickel plated round J hooks are chosen for their superior strength. The canted head of the round hook resists straightening from applied tension much better than flat hook designs most often seen on inexpensive import banjos.

9. The nickel plated Vega-style nut is used because it is both well made and extremely attractive.

10. Remo Renaissance high crown head is an accepted standard for open back use and works well in conjunction with the rolled brass tone ring. Head material is a very personal choice, but I find the "improvement" in tone by using natural hide just isn't worth the additional effort to keep it tensioned properly. As with my choice in neck reenforcement, I'd much rather put my time into playing vs. tweaking.

11. The multi-layered wood rim is capped with 3/16" Garolite XX opaque black phenolic material applied in 8 segments and edges rounded over for comfort. This jet black material is simply the best material I've ever found to simulate the look and feel of the finest ebony.

12. The neck design allows easy string height adjustment (even under tension) by simply loosening the single 1/4" by 20 acorn nut located below the tail piece bracket, repositioning the neck extension, and re-tightening the acorn nut.
I prefer my action low compared to most clawhammer players, and one of my friends stated that he personally liked a bit higher action when he was test-driving mine. I loosened the acorn nut, re-positioned the dowel slightly, re-tightened and handed it back to him. This took all of about 30 seconds and needless to say he was more than a little surprised by the speed and ease of making that adjustment. If you've ever wanted to do something as "simple" as trying out a taller bridge then you can begin to understand the usefulness of this design.

13. The fixed relationship of the neck extension (dowel stick) assures the strings will always center over the neck heel... plain and simple. Proper fitting of a neck heel is most likely the single most difficult part of banjo construction to get right. I've worked on a few brand-new banjos from well-known and respected small shop makers that shipped with strings that didn't center over the neck. With the neck through design I don't even think about it any longer. It doesn't get any better than that.

14. I most often do fret boards in Pau Ferro, a non-CITES listed wood. NO ebony is used in the Backstep banjo in consideration of the politics and nature of usage of rare exotics. Garolite XXX opaque black material (as well as "Richlite") can be used as an alternative to ebony but does require carbide tools to work it.

15. The double ogee scoop creates maximum thumb room for the scoop and is just plain beautiful on an otherwise unadorned instrument.

16. The arrangement of fifteen full and two partial frets is used in conjunction with the double ogee scoop to allow maximum right hand thumb clearance while retaining the upper notes most often used on the first two strings. EVO fret material is used for its high wear resistance combined with its ability to be easily worked.

17. The use of a "zero fret" ensures that the string height at this end of the neck is as low as possible; a requirement for ease of playing, and ensures that this critical height will be maintained over time. The zero fret also eliminates the time-consuming and finicky process of setting string clearances by hand cutting and shaping nut slots. Using the zero fret in conjunction with the synthetic string guide also allows steel or synthetic strings to be used interchangeably with no need to adjust nut slot width, as is often necessary when changing string materials on a conventional banjo.
My personal preference is Aquila Red series strings for beautiful tone and light under-hand feel, but I realize many prefer steel strings. It's nice to have the option to use both without the need to make any changes to the instrument.

18. "Three + two" peg head design, configured for either slot head or vertical string post orientation, just makes sense. The use of 3+2 tuner configuration ties into the tunneled neck design and the way that it removes the obstacle of the neck-mounted fifth string tuner at the fifth fret. Some feel that the neck-mount tuner is part of what defines the five string banjo, but the instrument sounds and functions identically with a tunneled neck; all of the advantages of a re-entrant fifth string with none of the drawbacks. The 3+2 tuner orientation quickly becomes second nature to the player. I've played both for a long time and I can tell you which feels more natural to the hand. Nuff said.

19. Grover Sta-tite Deluxe 18:1 cast base slot head tuners. The sooner you get over the idea that banjos have to have tuners that protrude directly out the peg head rear face the better off you're going to be. Good "right angle" tuners don't slip, are more accurate, and are more reliable than planetary tuners. Opponents of right angle tuners often state that tuning is slower with a high ratio tuner. It takes about 3/4 turn of the tuner button to raise or lower the pitch by a half step. Is that really slowing you down? I didn't think so.

20. The tunneled fifth string design uses stainless steel micro-tubing in an idealized path that minimizes any tendency to hold string tension due to friction within the tube. I love watching players unfamiliar with tunneled necks react to the streamlined neck profile. Once you play a tunneled neck you'll get it.

21. A small section of stainless steel is fitted over the 5th fret to serve as a micro-saddle. This raises the fifth string a few thousandths to prevent the 5th string from buzzing if plucked hard by the right thumb. It's not strictly necessary, but it causes no problem and most players like the tiny bit of added height.

22. A single spike is installed at the 7th fret to facilitate key changes with the use of a capo at the second fret. My preference is to divot the board where the spike is installed so the spike head is substantially below the level of the fret crowns. This works well for synthetic strings which require a slightly larger gap between the board surface and the underside of the spike head.

23. My preference is to reinforce my banjo necks in a manner that makes them rock solid and maintenance free. It makes no sense to remove a large portion of what stabilizes the neck and then work with mechanical contrivances to fix the problem you created. If the neck is made properly and includes reinforcement that CANNOT BE BENT BY HAND then you can forget seasonal movement or the need to intermittently adjust relief. Just as the arguments against the bumblebee being able to fly, I've followed with interest all of the reasons why my 2024-T4 bar can't work. Practical experience proves to me it does. It's really all about doing it the right way.
I prefer to spend my banjo time playing and not fuddling with neck adjustment.

24. I like the look of shell, even collecting and working old shell from local streams for my first few banjos. I chose to go with super-minimalist position markers and side dots for the Backstep design and love the resulting look. The use of synthetic material was also based on using woods that are not CITES listed or natural shell material that may be called into question as to its endangered status, either presently or in the foreseeable future. The simple synthetic markers work well with the entire Backstep ethic, based on functional design and simple elegance.

25. I've been using laminated bridge designs for several years and like both the aesthetic and the practicality. The laminated design makes for a stronger bridge at the same weight as a solid piece of wood and has no detrimental effect that I can hear.

26. The low profile contoured arm rest has been a feature on most of my banjos for quite a while. It is wide enough to provide a very comfortable rest for the forearm and thin enough to not change the arm position. Once I tried this design I knew I had found the ultimate rest; at least as far as I was concerned. You can read all of the specifics of the design in a topic I posted at Banjo Hangout "World's Best Armrest" where I had several folks try out a "kit" form of the rest.

My preferred setup is low action over the fret board and I prefer the less-agressive tonality of Aquila Red series strings. Here's a quick video demonstration of that setup performing Iry LeJeune's "Lacassine Special". Open back banjo... It's not just for old time!
