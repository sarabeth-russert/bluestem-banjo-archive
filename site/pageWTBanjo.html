Bluestem Wood Topped Banjo

Bluestem Wood Top Banjo features:

  • Lightly braced 1/8" spruce-topped 11" by 3" pot
  • Closed back body for superior sound
  • 1/8" mahogany back and matching 7/64" sides
  • Grover Sta-tite guitar-style tuners for easy tuning and great stability
  • Designed for use with steel strings
  • 25-1/2" scale length neck with 22 nickel silver frets and mother of pearl position markers
  • Three piece laminated neck reinforced with tapered 2024-T4 aircraft aluminum bar
  • Bolt on neck with channeled internal dowel stick for easy assembly
  • Entire banjo weighs in at 3 pounds 5 ounces!
There are many variations within the banjo family and the wood topped banjo has remained a consistent, although less well known presence throughout the banjo's colorful history.

The design shown here is based on an example that can be found listed as the "FM Franklin Banjo" at the Museum of Appalachia website; photos and details of the original instrument can be seen there. The instrument (record #91) can best be accessed from the museum's extensive collection of instruments by searching the database for "Franklin" (without quotation marks) in the search function box on the museum website.

Although a strong emphasis was placed on being true to the original design, the Bluestem Wood Top Banjo has a few modifications that increase the functionality of the example instrument.

The original tuners have been replaced with high grade Grover Sta-tite guitar-style tuners. One of the extra tuners from the guitar set that was purchased for the project was modified and used in place of the friction type fifth string tuner found on the original museum example instrument. Right angle geared tuners can be seen in both vertical and horizontal configurations on many banjos of past historical importance, so this serves as an ideal opportunity to demonstrate how to incorporate the bump out ledge for tuner mounting.

The neck utilizes my usual 2024-T4 aircraft aluminum bar reinforcement and is joined to the rim with an adaptation of my standard "all-thread through channeled dowel stick" design. The body features a permanently installed channeled dowel stick to house the neck mounting rod and a single 1/4"-20 acorn nut retains both the neck and the tailpiece. This neck attachment method also allows the neck to be removed or installed in a matter of minutes.

The closed back pot (as found on the original design) is also of note because the enclosed air chamber contributes to producing a superior sound.

I also make use of a modified low angle X brace for superior sound and to form the central arch in the instrument's top and back. The X bracing also serves to improve structural integrity, as the standard cross bracing normally found in wood top banjo designs inhibits the ability of the top to respond to seasonal humidity changes without the danger of cracking the spruce top. An instrument such as the guitar has a side profile that allows the top to expand and contract, but the circular form of the banjo limits the ability of the top and back to accommodate these seasonal dimensional changes. Some wood top banjo designs attempt to deal with these structural issues by using plywood for the top, but this also contributes to poor sound quality.

Assorted photos of the construction process

I've chosen not to present this plan as part of a complete building guide, so I'm alternatively presenting several shots of the building process with a bit of information for each photo to assist anyone interested in constructing a similar instrument. Critical measurements and other assorted construction tips are presented on the actual full size plan.

The Wood Top Banjo was built concurrently with a Tenor Ukulele build and many of the basic construction techniques are similar. Readers interested in learning more about the construction methods and techniques used should also refer to the Ukulele page HERE for tips on how I thickness the sides, top and back, join top and back halves, cut kerfed lining strips, do decorative end wedge joints, and more.

Forming the channeled dowel stick



A pair of small wedges are inserted in the ends of the channeled dowel stick halves to hold them in alignment until the glue sets up a bit. As soon as the glue has set up enough to prevent the sides from shifting under clamping pressure the wedges can be removed and a bit of moistened paper towel pushed through the channel "ram rod style" to remove excess glue.

The construction mold and basic rim components



1. Wood form, 1-1/2” thick, 11” inside diameter, 15” outside diameter
2. Two sides, 3” by 20” by 7/64”
3. Head and tail blocks, all 4 edges relieved to form 3/8” glue ledge
4. Four 12” lengths of 3/8” by 1/4” reverse kerf lining strips
5. Two 3/32” by 3/8” wide side reinforcing strips
6. Dowel stick cross section example
7. 1-3/8” square dowel stick with 3/8” by 5/8” internal channel
8. Five piece decorative wedge over end joint of rim sides
9. Number 6 trim screws reinforce dowel stick to head and tail block glue joints
10. Signature and date area visible through top lyre cut out

Top glued on & back ready to glue to rim assembly



1. Arched Sitka spruce top (1/8") & mahogany back (1/8")
2. Arched spruce (for top) and walnut X braces with parabolic end taper
3. Circular thin spruce cross grain center seam reinforcement
4. “F” Hole and center Lyre cutouts
5. Cross grain reinforcement
6. Cross grain center seam reinforcement
7. Information label
8. Rim edges sanded to convex shape to match plate arching
9. Light pencil marking to assist with glue application
10. Temporary locating cleats to assist with gluing

Trimming top overhang



The top overhang is cut within 1/8" of the rim and trimmed flush with a bearing guided router bit before the back is glued on. It is done prior to the back being added so the band saw can be used in a manner that prevents any possibility of chip out or splintering of the spruce top. If the back was already glued the band saw could possibly cause chip out or splintering of the rear face. Both edges could be sanded and rounded over after the back is glued, but I'm going to cut binding ledges and add contrasting wood binding after the back is glued on.

Stewart-MacDonald binding router base

Kinda...

Stewart-MacDonald sold this replacement "tool mounting bar" for $15 and I had an extra Dremel, so I used it to make a dedicated binding ledge router. The base is sanded so it has a very slight taper to the outside with the high point running along the mounting bolt centerline. In use this accommodates the top arch nicely; just hold it at the bolt locations and keep the high centerline roughly parallel to the top ledge. It works great, has a nice balance point, and frees up my "good" Stew-Mac base for other uses.


Composite neck photo

Here's a composite photo of the peg head and heel end of the neck. When it's all said and done the center lamination of the neck blank will be centered at the peg head point, the nut, and the heel as depicted here.
The hole for the barrel nut can be drilled 13/32" as indicated here, or alternatively 25/64" as shown on the actual plan. The slightly smaller 25/64" results in a better match to the barrel nut, but it's not worth purchasing a special drill bit if you don't have the 25/64" available.
The actual details of the fret board design, layout, and how it relates to the neck blank center line are featured on the "Open Back Banjo Design Primer" page. I felt that this was a good opportunity to use the design and layout of the fret board and how it relates to the laminated neck blank to clarify details of neck construction that can be difficult to grasp without a good visual representation.



Peg head overlay

The peg head used on this banjo has a fairly complex shape, so I opted to go with a simple peg head overlay to balance the design elements somewhat. The abundance of scrap in my ebony bin contributed to the decision to do the simple wedge design shown here. The sections are edge matched, assembled on "sticky-side-up" packing tape, and the thin wood wedge (far right) is tapped in to exert clamping force while the glue dries.



Relationship of peghead, nut, string, and fretboard centers

Here is a shot of how the nut end of the fretboard, the peg head overlay, and the neck blank will be positioned. Notice that the neck blank is centered perfectly on the peghead, nut, and neck locations, no need to do any special positioning of any of the key components. You can easily see that mounting the neck with the third string centerline aligned with the pot centerline resolves all of those sticky neck alignment issues.



Preliminary assembly

The neck heel is mated to the body at the correct angle and rearward slope using the vertical oscillating spindle sander and heel profiling jig shown on the banjo construction information page.
It is necessary to profile the heel before the neck is shaped because it would not be possible to use the vertical oscillating spindle sanding jig due to the small fretboard projection which extends beyond the heel and over the body. The neck heel is locked to the body by screwing a section of 1/4"-20 all thread into the barrel bolt embedded in the neck heel, passing it through the channeled dowel stick and securing it with a washer and nut at the tailpiece end of the body.

The top portion of the photo demonstrates the "TRUE" centerline which runs from the center of the tailpiece location, directly over the heel center and meets the third string nut slot center.

The bottom portion of the photo shows that a straightedge placed at the nut and raised 1/8" at the heel produces a gap of 5/8" at the bridge position if the back angle of the heel is cut correctly. Verifying this angle as correct will ensure that the string clearances and action will be correct once the frets are installed and a bridge is added. The neck heel back angle needs to be fairly close on this banjo due to the fretboard overhang that extends past the edge of the body. The neck could be easily raised to lower the string clearance over the upper frets, but it would be much more difficult to raise the action by lowering the neck should that be desirable in the future.

Note that the fretboard is NOT attached in the photo; it is only temporarily resting on top of the neck blank. Once the back angle is verified (and modified if necessary) it can be glued to the neck blank.



Refining the neck

Fretboard glued on, peghead overlay added, tuner holes drilled, neck shaped, and fifth string tuner ledge defined using a Dremel milling cutter in the drill press.

The peghead string post holes were drilled 9/32" for clearance; the holes will be later reamed from the face side of the peghead to accommodate the press-in string post ferrules.

The fifth string tuner (visible in the photo next to an unmodified tuner for comparison) has the string post shortened to 5/8" and a new 1/16" string hole has been drilled. This tuner will protrude only a small amount above the fretboard so it won't cause any problems when fretting. With the shortened string post and no ferrule used, the fifth string tuner hole is drilled to a snug 1/4" fit to prevent the force of the tensioned string from pulling it sideways.



Sanding the peghead outline

The majority of the preliminary sanding of the neck was done with 220 grit sandpaper on a foot-controlled 5" random orbit sander. The peghead requires a bit of hand work due to its complex profile. Various shapes of sanding sticks have 220 grit sandpaper attached with double-faced carpet tape are used to smooth the rough sawn edges.
The neck is now ready for fret installation followed by final sanding. Final sanding before finish application will take care of any small finish imperfections that might occur when the neck is handled during the fretting process.



Final preliminaries

The frets were installed and a custom brass tailpiece was made. The only thing left to do is join the neck to the body and add the nut and bridge. Here's a composite photo showing string locations being transferred to the bridge and nut with the "Bluestem Banjo String Spacing Guide" which is available for free download in pdf form in the "Open Back Banjo Design Primer" section.



Design details

Here is a composite photo showing a few of the instrument design details. It is assembled here to check how everything fits together, especially important for a new design. If everything is satisfactory it will be disassembled for sanding and finish application.

The tuners are installed, but the string post bushings are left 1/8" shy from being seated so they can be easily removed for final sanding and finish application. Notice that the strings are wound to the outside of the string posts. This is done so the buttons will turn in the normal direction to raise or lower string pitch. They were installed in this manner to match the appearance of the museum example, but details are included on the plan if it is desired to position the buttons above the string post as found on today's designs. I actually like the looks of the button-under peghead design as seen here.

The fifth string tuner is reminiscent of the tuners seen on the Ashborn designs from the mid-1800's and work really well. The string post is about 1/8" higher than the fretboard surface and the hole is chamfered to provide a bit of additional clearance to attach the string. Standard fare for the heel and heel cap, with the neck to body connection being rock solid due to the channeled internal dowel stick neck to body attachment method.

The instrument is designed to have string pressure bear over the X bracing, so the three footed bridge was replaced with the two footed design as shown here.

The tailpiece and end bracket are shaped from brass and allow the opportunity to add a bit of extra weight to help the overall instrument balance. The completed banjo weighs in at 3 pounds 5 ounces and balances nicely with the center of gravity being at the curve of the heel. As a comparison, my standard open back designs come in at a bit over 5 pounds.

Completed Wood Top banjo:







I'm quite pleased with how this project turned out.

It plays like a dream, has good neck balance, and a tonality much more like a dulcimer than banjo. I knew the sound would be mellow before starting the project, and I'm very satisfied with the pleasant bell-like tone. Volume is ample, although certainly a bit lower than a standard banjo design with a stretched membrane head.
