The homestead:

Front view of the old homestead. The porch wraps all the way around so I can always find a shady spot to play music. The back porch is even screened for bug-free afternoon and evening playing!



We're situated on 7 acres with a separate 2-1/2 car garage/shop and a 24' by 32' machine shed.


The shop:

Here is my latest version of an economical and compact shop. I've worked with various configurations over the years and this is the best so far. Organized wood storage rack against the back wall, bulk lumber storage area in the shed, and separate humidity-controlled area in the house for pickup winding and final assembly work are not shown.

Trip hazards are not acceptable in the shop!



First thing to equip any shop with is a SOLID workbench. I threw this one together over a weekend and liked it so well it hasn't changed much in the last 10 years. Rigid workbenches such as this one will always rock a tiny bit on the uneven floor, so there's a wooden wedge that is kicked in under the high foot to totally eliminate movement. The wedge can be seen under the back left foot in this photo. The bench has a full width end vise that utilizes under-bench pipe clamps to tension it. Dual foot releases for the pipe clamp vise are located at the base of the far leg in the photo, although all you can see in the photo are the release cables that go to the foot levers. The full width end vise and bench top have rows of 3/4" holes so conventional bench dogs can be used to secure work against the bench top. The entire bench works exceptionally well.
The machinist's vise can be mounted or removed in seconds with a 6" C-clamp. The small tools holder is also a virtual necessity; it has made my bench life so much more enjoyable. Outlets and a good source of light are also high points. A foot activated switch hangs from the rear leg and is most often used in conjunction with the 5" random orbit disk sander. Pulsing the switch with a toe allows extremely controllable sanding work. On nice days I run the switch to the deck outside for lots of sunshine and fresh air. Electrical cords drop down from ceiling mounted receptacles.
Don't forget to make yourself a nice place to sit and get a few cushioned mats for tasks that require standing.



Organized storage is important; jigs are stored on top and are not visible in the photo. A solid roll-around auxiliary work surface comes in handy; here it holds my fretting saw which is covered more thoroughly on the construction tips page. The table saw gets used for the occasional furniture projects but I could build instruments without it. After this photo was taken I "upgraded" to a smaller DeWalt benchtop 10" on a custom stand. A small 6" stand grinder sees occasional use, as well as the small anvil that pays homage to my dad, who was a blacksmith by trade. This was his small anvil for home use and it makes me smile. The leaf blower is another necessity; open the overhead door and you can have a clean shop in five minutes time!
Man does not live by banjo alone, so the barbeque grill holds an honored position in the shop. (I do roll it out on the deck to use it, though...)



Hanging from the ceiling is a box fan with a simple filter cage that holds a pre-filter and a fine particulate filter. This works remarkably well to keep the fine dust down to a minimal amount and is quick and cheap to build. The filters simply lift up over the frame edge to remove for cleaning or replacement.

Closing remarks:

The information shown here attempts to show how I have "tooled up" to suit my needs and finances. I could have a larger shop and more tools, but I really like working in a simple manner with a "minimalist" collection of tools. I can do everything I want to do with the tools shown here; in a few cases I've purchased larger and "better" tools and sold them because I prefer the smaller versions. If I can't move something by myself I have no use for it. I make more than instruments and the house is furnished with several larger pieces of furniture I've made, so the tools are not an obstacle to the work at hand for me.

The bottom line is that each of us finds our own way to the work methodology that we are comfortable with and it truly is an individual journey. DO NOT throw your hands up in dispair and think that you'll never be able to equip yourself to build instruments, there are MANY acceptable paths to the pursuit of luthiery.

If you are just beginning your pursuit of any craft best advice is to start at the beginning. That might sound a bit too obvious, but it's an important concept to grasp. if you want to build banjos (or anything else for that matter) the best thing to do is to START and then systematically work to eliminate each obstacle you find in your path. At the end you'll have the satisfaction I've found in playing instruments that you can proudly say you've created with your own hands. It doesn't get any better than that.
