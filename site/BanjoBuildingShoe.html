Step By Step Guide To The Design And Construction Of A Complete Open Back Banjo

Shoes

In a standard open back banjo configuration the shoes are attached to the rim and serve as the anchor point for the J hook. The tensioning nuts bear against the shoe and pull the tension band down to stretch the head over the top of the rim.

1. Making your own raw brass shoes

Although it may not be cost-effective, making your own raw brass hardware can be quite satisfying. I personally like the look of raw brass shoes and tension band when combined with nickel plated J hooks and nuts. I also like the ability to make the shoes in a way that keeps the shoes and related hardware as close as possible to the rim to minimise discomfort. Many open backs are uncomfortable to play due to the protruding hardware pressing into the leg and I like to minimize that as much as possible.

The first step in making a set of raw brass shoes is to create a pattern which will be used to lay them out on brass bar stock.
This shoe design works well. The 3/8" by 1" mounting surface provides good support to keep the shoe from heeling in over time.
The position of the mounting screw draws the shoe firmly against the rim and the cantilevered force of the J hook is supported by the upper portion of the shoe.
The precise location of the holes ensures that there are no blind holes to deal with and the shoe can be held with a screw inserted from the front.
The shape and rounding are all calculated to produce an attractive shoe with the least amount of time and materials.



A fine point permanent marker is used to mark each pair of shoes on both ends of 3/8" by 1/2" brass bar stock.
This method allows you to do identical machining steps on 4 shoes at a time for each section of brass bar stock you have available.



The curved portion of the shoe profile is removed by bandsawing away the waste area.
I use a 3 TPI 1/2" skip tooth bi-metal bandsaw blade for ALL my shop work and it cuts the brass easily.
I make 5 equally spaced cuts down to the line to make the curved portion easier to cut with the 1/2" wide blade.



A vertical oscillating spindle sander with a 2" diameter 80 grit drum is used to refine the shape of the profile, working carefully to the line.
Since both ends of the bar have been marked and rough cut that means 4 shoes are being rough cut and sanded.



Each pair of shoes are trimmed from the bar and the ends sanded square to produce shoes with a consistent 1/8" lip at the front edge.
Here 20 shoes are prepared and you can see that they are very uniform in shape.



The previously squared and sanded ends are marked to indicate the distance from the rear edge for the 3/16" J hook hole to be drilled.
The notch depth of 1/16" positions the center line of the 3/16" J hook hole 5/16" out from the rear mounting face.



Since the pattern is made from the same 3/8" thick bar stock as the shoes it can be used to mark the location of the mounting screw hole.



The pattern is used to mark the final 1" length of each shoe.



Each end of each pair of shoes has the 3/16" J hook hole drilled.
The centering of the hole across the width isn't marked as it's easy enough to eyeball the center when the holes are drilled.



The hook pairs are next drilled 5/32" for the tapped #10-32 mounting holes.



As each pair of hooks are drilled the tap is placed in the drill press chuck and a bit of bees wax lubricant is added to the tap end.
The toothbrush is used to clear debris from the tap gullets.



The tap is started in each hole SPINNING THE DRILL PRESS CHUCK BY HAND ONLY.
This ensures the threads are started in perfect alignment with the drilled holes.
The tap only needs a thread of two to hold it in place while the chuck is loosened and raised.



Attaching a T handle tap wrench allows the tapping of the hole to be finished by hand.
The tap is advanced until it becomes tight and then backed off a turn to break and clear the chip before advancing forward again.
You'll most likely hear squeaking as you tap; don't be alarmed if you're not accustomed to working brass. It's quite normal.



Here a #10-32 screw is shown threaded into a tapped hole.
Notice that a good design allows easy drilling and tapping with no "blind" holes to deal with.



A simple wood jig is used to safely hold the shoes so they can be trimmed to length.



The small bit of excess material is trimmed away from each shoe in the same manner.
Here a small block is used to safely hold the shoe in position, keeping the hands well away from the cutting area.



The curved portion of the pattern is used to mark a consistent curved layout line on each shoe.



Both the top end and the front edge are sanded to produce a slightly curved edge and the corners are also rounded over just a bit.



Here you can see that all of the shoes are very uniform in shape.
Placing them all side by side makes it very easy to spot any shoe that might need just a bit of additional sanding.



All that careful design pays off!
The location of the tapped hole allows the shoe to be held by means of a long #10-32 screw inserted from the front side of the shoe.
The rear face of each shoe is contour sanded with a 3" diameter drum so they will fit tightly against the rim when mounted.
Small details like the contouring of the mounting face are the "icing on the cake" of a finely crafted instrument.



Each shoe is sanded with a 220 grit disk fitted to the 5" random orbit sander to refine sanding marks left earlier to prepare for final polishing.
There's a quick video of the sanding operation on my YouTube channel showing the fine control of the sander using the momentary foot switch.
Enter ramblinrudy1 into the YouTube search box to access my YouTube videos.



The shoes are held with a simple wood handle with a long #10-32 screw inserted through it that holds the shoe while it is polished.
There is a complete video of the polishing operation on my YouTube channel.
Enter ramblinrudy1 into the YouTube search box to access my YouTube videos.



The polished shoes are swirled a bit in the patina solution shown in the previous photo.
A bit of hand buffing with a clean cotton cloth will finish these up before being mounted to the rim.

2. Shoe options

Another option is to purchase commercially available shoes from one of the several vendors who sell banjo hardware on the internet. They are easy to contact if you do a quick Google search for them.

Here are a few suppliers of banjo hardware ranked according to my experience and personal knowledge:

1. Stewart-MacDonald
Stew-Mac carries many banjo parts and offers the ubiquitous cast "L-shoe". This small and unattractive shoe is the same style as most of the eBay offerings. I buy a lot of tuning machines from Stew-Mac but not much else.

2. Bill Rickard
Rickard Banjos has become one of the world's best suppliers of QUALITY banjo components, and even has a line of completed "Mapleleaf" banjos. Bill is a true force of nature and a great guy who deserves a lot of admiration for many reasons, the least of which being his GREAT banjo hardware.



3. Balsam Banjoworks
Balsam Banjoworks is the parts division of Pisgah Banjos and their mission is to make available a complete line of American-made hardware to the individual and small shop builder. Good stuff!



4. Bob Smakula
Bob has many items available, is very pleasant to deal with, has heaps of banjo knowledge, and has customer service second to none.

5. Elderly Music
Elderly has several items for banjo construction available and carries my favorite Vega-style open ended nuts with good bulk pricing. Also a good source for Aquila Nylgut strings if you use them.

6. There are many more suppliers of hardware like American Made, Ant Hill Music, Greg Boyd, etc. that I have no personal knowledge of so can't offer an opinion.

7. Ebay
I don't shop Ebay for banjo hardware, most of the offerings are of variable (read as poor...) quality. Competitive pricing means most Ebay stuff is really based on how cheaply it can be made and you can figure out what that means for you as the consumer. The majority of the cost of an instrument is the labor, so it makes no sense to me to use inferior hardware.
